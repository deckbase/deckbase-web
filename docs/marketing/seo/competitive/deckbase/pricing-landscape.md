## Value Metric Analysis

**What each competitor charges for (and why):**

### Quizlet — charges for AI access + ad removal
The key unlock in Quizlet Plus is removing AI restrictions (Magic Notes, Q-Chat) and ads. The value metric is "remove friction from the free experience" rather than "unlock more studying." This creates a natural ceiling — users with minimal friction don't upgrade.

### Anki — charges once for iOS access, nothing else
Unique in the market: a one-time payment model. This is structurally opposed to subscription monetization. Revenue is tied to iOS downloads, not usage retention. No upsell path exists. The model succeeds because of Anki's power-user loyalty, not pricing sophistication.

### Knowt — charges for AI generation volume + ad removal
Knowt Ultra's gating is primarily AI generation limits (the most-demanded feature in the market). Even Ultra has monthly caps [Data — Fritz.ai, 2025]. This creates frustration for heavy users and is a structural weakness Deckbase can exploit by offering truly unlimited AI generation at the same or lower price.

### Brainscape — charges for certified deck library access
The primary lock-in is their curated deck library (MCAT, bar exam, language packs). This is a different value metric: pre-made content access, not tool capability. Deckbase doesn't compete on this axis — it competes on AI-generated custom content.

### RemNote — charges for document import limits
3 PDFs on the free tier is a very low cap. The value metric is "depth of note/document workflow." Relevant only for users who are document-heavy (medical school, research). Deckbase's equivalent is not capped by document count.

### Mochi — charges for card count limits
Classic freemium gate on the core product metric (number of flashcards). Clean and predictable but doesn't reflect how users experience value — they don't count cards, they value recall quality.

## Pricing Psychology Breakdown

### Quizlet
- **Anchoring:** Plus Unlimited at $9.99/mo makes $7.99/mo Plus feel reasonable
- **Decoy pricing:** Three tiers (Free / Plus / Plus Unlimited) with Plus as the target
- **Charm pricing:** $35.99/yr (not $36); $7.99/mo (not $8)
- **Annual commitment discount:** 63% off monthly rate if paying annually — strong lock-in pressure
- **7-day free trial:** Reduces switching anxiety but commits users to annual billing
- **Psychology grade: B+** — well-executed standard SaaS playbook, but declining "-18% YoY" on "quizlet plus" suggests lower conversion than expected

### Anki
- **One-time purchase signal:** Appeals to power users who distrust subscriptions
- **iOS price shock:** $24.99 is jarring vs. expectations for an app (which most users expect to be $0–$4.99); triggers negative reviews
- **No pricing psychology:** There is no upsell, no annual vs. monthly, no decoy — which is itself a differentiator for a specific user type
- **Psychology grade: C** — works for the niche, creates a recurring acquisition leak for competitors

### Knowt
- **Free-first acquisition:** Generous free tier mirrors Quizlet's strategy
- **Quizlet price comparison:** At ~$60/yr, Knowt is actually MORE expensive than Quizlet ($35.99/yr) when paid — but positioning is "free Quizlet alternative" which hides this
- **No trial mentioned** — users discover pricing only when hitting limits
- **Psychology grade: C+** — good free tier, but paid positioning is confused (they're cheaper at free but more expensive at paid)

### Brainscape
- **Science-backed authority:** "Proven to DOUBLE learning results" justifies a price premium
- **Certified library as value anchor:** Pre-made professional decks serve as social proof for the paid tier
- **B2B enterprise upmarket:** Having a custom enterprise tier makes the individual plan look affordable
- **Psychology grade: B** — solid for its niche, but aging strategy; no AI to refresh the value proposition

### RemNote
- **Feature gate on productivity workflow:** 3 PDF imports is a pain-point trigger for their core user (heavy note-takers)
- **$6/mo is low but not aggressive** — doesn't stand out vs. $5/mo alternatives
- **Psychology grade: C+** — the gate works but the price positioning is undifferentiated

## Pricing Whitespace

### 1. Sub-Quizlet annual pricing with superior features
**Opportunity:** Quizlet charges $35.99/yr for a weaker feature set (no FSRS, AI geo-restricted). Positioning Deckbase at $24.99/yr is meaningfully cheaper than Quizlet, while offering FSRS + AI + rich media + offline.

**Why it works:** Students choosing between apps primarily compare price + feature parity. At $24.99/yr vs. $35.99/yr, Deckbase is 30% cheaper — a real "why not switch?" argument.

### 2. Monthly option to capture Anki iOS refugees
**Opportunity:** Anki iOS costs $24.99 one-time. A $4.99/mo subscription with a free trial offers lower barrier to entry for users who want modern UX + AI but resist the upfront cost.

**Why it works:** "I can try it for free first" vs. "I have to pay $24.99 upfront for an app that looks like it's from 2008" is an easy sell. Monthly churn risk is offset by Deckbase's unique FSRS history moat — users who invest in their FSRS schedule are unlikely to cancel.

### 3. Unlimited AI generation as a differentiator
**Opportunity:** Every competitor either excludes AI from the free tier (Quizlet geo-restriction), limits it at all paid tiers (Knowt Ultra caps), or doesn't have it (Anki, Brainscape). Positioning truly unlimited AI generation in the paid tier (or generous limits in free) is a concrete differentiator users can feel.

**Why it works:** The top customer frustration is AI generation limits. "Unlimited AI cards from your notes" as a hero feature on the pricing page directly converts users hitting Knowt Ultra's caps.

### 4. Free tier with FSRS (unique in the market)
**Opportunity:** Anki is the only other app offering real FSRS for free — but Anki requires desktop setup. A mobile-first free tier with real FSRS (no algorithm downgrade) is unique. Quizlet, Knowt, and Brainscape all use inferior SRS algorithms at all tiers.

- **Hardest converts:** Quizlet users with large social libraries — social network effect can't be replicated
